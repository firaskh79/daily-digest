This Week's Signal

Britain after Starmer

Keir Starmer resigned as Prime Minister on Monday 22 June, less than two years after Labour's landslide. The proximate cause was Andy Burnham's return to Parliament as MP for Makerfield, which formalized what the numbers already showed: he had the nominations to trigger a contest and Starmer chose to leave rather than fight. The leadership contest opens 9 July. Burnham is running; no credible centrist has declared, which may mean coronation is more likely than contest. What's striking is how quiet the market reaction was. Sterling fell to around $1.32, gilt yields ticked up briefly then settled near 4.85% on the 10-year. Analysts noted the pound had already lost around 3% since February as the challenge became inevitable. Britain just changed its Prime Minister and the gilt market barely flinched. That calm is partly about Burnham pledging to keep the current fiscal rules, and partly about how thoroughly the political crisis was priced in. The interesting question is whether Burnham's instincts — northern, public-service focused, economically interventionist — eventually register somewhere the market hasn't yet caught.

Ukraine changes gear

On Thursday 26 June, Ukraine launched a 660-drone overnight assault on at least 12 Russian regions — one of the largest single attacks of the war. This wasn't a standalone event. Zelenskyy had formally announced a 40-day SBU "influence operation" aimed at compelling Russia to end the war, explicitly framing military pressure as the primary lever after a year of failed US mediation. The framing matters: this is Kyiv saying diplomacy isn't working and it is choosing escalation deliberately. Tatiana Stanovaya, a Moscow-based analyst whose work is worth knowing, offered the more uncomfortable version of the same picture: Russia's military advantage is dissipating and Putin's actual escalatory options are extremely limited — nuclear use risks everything, conventional escalation risks more losses he can't absorb. Her read is that Putin is not in a dominant position but a compelled one. That's a more unsettling framing than Western triumphalism suggests, and a more honest one. Whether a compelled actor stays rational across 40 days of pressure is the question.

Ebola reaches France

On 24 June, France confirmed a case of Ebola Bundibugyo — the first confirmed case outside Africa since the outbreak began. The DRC case count has risen from 837 to 1,155 in the past seven days; deaths from 196 to 304. Uganda has been clear for three weeks, which is good. But a case in France is precisely the international spread signal the WHO has been warning about since declaring a Public Health Emergency of International Concern in May. There is still no approved Bundibugyo vaccine. The WHO rates the current response effort at roughly three to four out of ten. This story competed for almost no oxygen in a week dominated by Starmer and Ukraine. It deserved more.

Ukraine — Zelenskyy's 40-Day Operation Changed

The 660-drone attack was the headline but the strategic declaration is the story. Zelenskyy has given the SBU's deep-strike campaign an explicit political deadline and purpose: 40 days to impose enough cost that Moscow moves toward ending the war. The campaign has been degrading Russian energy infrastructure and supply chains for months — this formalises the intent as a political, not merely military, operation. Crimea entered a state of emergency. Russia claimed to down all 660 drones, a figure Western analysts treat with scepticism given reports of fires at energy facilities across 12 regions. Moscow has threatened escalation, and Christopher Granville at TS Lombard called it a genuine end-game moment. Stanovaya's read is that Putin's escalatory options are nearly exhausted, which makes him predictable in the wrong direction.

Watch: Russian targeting of Ukrainian civilian infrastructure in retaliation; any NATO Article 4 consultation; whether the 40-day clock produces diplomatic back-channel movement by mid-July.
Iran / Hormuz — Roadmap Agreed, Inspections Disputed Changed

The Burgenstock talks produced a formal roadmap on 22 June: Iran has invited IAEA inspectors back, the US issued a 60-day oil sanctions waiver, and a communication line focused on keeping the Strait of Hormuz open has been established. Brent fell approximately 10% on the week to around $72 — the lowest since February — as Hormuz risk unwound. The fragility sits just beneath the surface: the US and Iran are in open disagreement about whether Tehran agreed to IAEA access to the bombed nuclear sites at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. Iran's deputy foreign minister says this is contingent on a final agreement and sanctions relief. The IAEA Director General says inspections will happen. The 440kg of uranium enriched to 60% that Iran held before the conflict cannot be verified until someone gets in. The 60-day clock is ticking on an inspection regime that may not exist in the form the US described.

Watch: Whether IAEA inspection modalities are publicly announced; Brent's response if the inspection impasse signals deal fragility; Lebanon MOU provisions (still unreleased).
South Africa — Jun 30 Deadline Changed

The anti-migrant deadline expires today. The South African government has deployed the military to secure airports and strategic sites; police have pre-emptively stated they will not tolerate post-deadline violence. At least two people have been killed in preceding weeks; Nigeria, Ghana, Mozambique, Malawi, and Zimbabwe have all run repatriation flights. The Ramaphosa government's posture is acceleration of deportations — co-opting the movement's demand rather than confronting it legally. The ISS Africa analysis is worth reading: the policing strategy is explicitly about preventing a single mass-casualty event rather than dismantling the movement behind the ultimatum. Cross-border trade is down approximately 22%. That doesn't recover quickly regardless of what happens in the next 24 hours.

Watch: Events in Johannesburg, Cape Town, and border areas today and tomorrow; AU or UNHCR formal response; trade data in coming weeks.
Ebola Bundibugyo — DRC / France Changed

Case count: 1,155 confirmed in DRC (from 837 a week ago), 304 deaths (from 196). Uganda has reported no new cases since 5 June. The France case confirmed 24 June almost certainly came via a recent traveller. A US citizen was medically evacuated to Germany in May; the France case makes two in high-income countries. The DRC outbreak is the source and it is not slowing. Ituri province accounts for 1,054 of the confirmed cases across 22 health zones. The WHO rates the response effort at three to four out of ten. Four hundred beds are available; the labs can process 1,000 samples a day; the WHO's own assessment is that this remains far below what is needed. Without an approved Bundibugyo vaccine, containment depends entirely on contact tracing and isolation capacity, which is severely strained.

Watch: Additional cases in Europe; DRC trajectory over the next fortnight; vaccine pipeline announcement; WHO raising alert level.
Colombia — De la Espriella Presidency New

Abelardo de la Espriella won Colombia's presidential runoff on 21 June by a much narrower margin than polls predicted — 49.66% against Ivan Cepeda's 48.70%, confirmed by the National Electoral Council on 24 June. Polls had shown an 8-point lead; the result was under one point. Colombia has elected its first far-right president: Trump-endorsed, committed to ending peace talks with rebel groups, restoring ties with Israel (including moving the embassy to Jerusalem), boosting oil and gas, and reducing the state by up to 40%. This is another node in Latin America's rightward shift alongside Milei and Bukele. The razor-thin margin will constrain him — he has no prior political experience, faces a divided Congress, and governs a country where the left took power four years ago. The Atlantic Council notes that rebuilding US-Colombia security cooperation is likely the first practical priority, with last week's US strike on the Tren de Aragua leader in Venezuela already signalling the direction of travel.

Watch: Cabinet appointments; initial posture on FARC talks; formal restoration of Israel ties; Colombia's economic response to expected debt headwinds.

Outside the Frame

New Voice · Tatiana Stanovaya — R.Politik

The dominant frame on Ukraine's 40-day operation is one of Ukrainian strength and justified pressure. Stanovaya, a Moscow-based political analyst who founded R.Politik, offers the more useful counter-read. Her argument is that Russia's military advantage is beginning to dissipate — and that for Putin, "escalation is the only way to respond to a situation you can't control." This is not a hawk's framing. It is an analyst who understands Kremlin decision-making saying that a cornered actor with no good options is not a stable actor. Her point is not that Putin will escalate rationally, but that he may have no other move. That framing sits uncomfortably alongside Zelenskyy's explicit 40-day pressure campaign. The optimistic read on the 40-day operation is that it forces Moscow toward the table. The Stanovaya read is that it forces Moscow toward the wall. Those are very different outcomes. Her analysis is available at r-politik.ru and via Riddle Russia — worth adding to the regular rotation alongside the ISW daily assessments.
