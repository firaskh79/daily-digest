Outside the Frame

Alastair Crooke — "Is the Ceasefire Dead?" / "America's Suez Crisis" (June 10, ScheerPost)

Published the day before Iran's most direct strike on US military infrastructure, Crooke's June 10 analysis argued Iran had shifted to "escalatory deterrence": match any US strike with a step up the ladder, not a step sideways. That prediction was borne out within 24 hours. His deeper structural frame — "America's Suez Crisis" — argues the US is in Britain's 1956 position: capable of striking, strategically unable to achieve its objectives, with costs mounting and allies uncomfortable. Britain hit Egypt in 1956 and won the battle; the US forced it to withdraw anyway and British imperial authority never recovered. Crooke is arguing the US-Iran war is following that structural path. His specific insight on the political trap: Netanyahu cannot remain in power without continuing to attack Lebanon, and "that is the real problem facing Trump." The June 22 talks are not between two parties that want a deal on the same terms — they are between a party that needs to stay at war (Israel) and a party that needs to be seen ending it (Trump), with Iran running the clock on both.

Trita Parsi (Quincy Institute) — "Trump Is Not in a Dominant Position" (Democracy Now, Substack)

Parsi's consistent frame since April offers a useful counter to both triumphalist and catastrophist readings of today's exchange. His argument: Trump "backed down" from attacking Iranian power plants because doing so would send oil above $200 and devastate the Gulf allies now being used as Iranian missile targets. The US cannot escalate its way out of this conflict. What looks like strength — day-2 strikes — may actually be an acknowledgment of the ceiling: the strikes are calibrated to stay below the threshold that would trigger a response too large to absorb politically and economically. If Parsi is right, the June 10-11 exchange is not the beginning of a genuine escalatory spiral but two parties signalling within a bounded game — simultaneously less catastrophic than it looks and more structurally broken than a genuine ceasefire would be.
