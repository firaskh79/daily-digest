Today's Signal

Oil below $100 — but the Iran deal is still a pressure tactic, not a peace

Brent crude dropped below $100 a barrel today on reports that the US-Iran MOU is "largely negotiated" and that Iran has begun coordinating controlled passage through the Strait of Hormuz. From above $105 last week to sub-$100 today — that's a move that matters for UK energy import costs and for gilt yields, which have fallen below 4.9% on the 10-year for the first time since late April.

But read the details before updating the model. The draft agreement commits Iran to "never pursue nuclear weapons" and to negotiate a suspension of enrichment — but Iranian officials have publicly denied making any commitment on enriched uranium stockpiles. The US continued airstrikes on Iranian positions yesterday. And Netanyahu announced he will intensify Lebanon strikes, directly contradicting Iran's demand that a Lebanon ceasefire be part of any deal. Lebanese parliament speaker Nabih Berri told Asharq Al-Awsat that Lebanon is included in the deal. Netanyahu says it isn't. Both things cannot be true. The Strait is reopening while the war is widening, and that tension is not a paradox — it is the negotiation.

The structural counter-frame: Richard Haass's argument (on his Substack "Home & Away", flagged last week) that Trump is functionally recreating the JCPOA he destroyed — and that maximum pressure produced a more hardline Iranian government, not a more pliable one. That framing looks sharper today, not weaker.

Murrell guilty plea: the SNP era ends in a courtroom

Peter Murrell — former SNP chief executive, estranged husband of Nicola Sturgeon — pleaded guilty on Monday to embezzling £400,310 from the SNP over thirteen years (2010–2023). He has been remanded in custody; sentencing is 23 June. The money funded luxury goods and vehicles. Sturgeon was cleared of wrongdoing last year, but Murrell and Sturgeon were the operational core of the independence movement at its peak. The guilty plea closes one chapter — but the sentencing hearing will reopen it. Watch whether Sturgeon speaks before June 23, and whether the SNP, already losing ground to Labour in Scotland, absorbs this or fractures further.

Ebola numbers jumped significantly overnight

The Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in DRC and Uganda has reached 1,010 suspected cases and 231 deaths as of 24 May — up from 746 cases and 176 deaths just two days earlier. Cases in Kampala are confirmed. There is no approved vaccine or treatment for the Bundibugyo strain. The outbreak is accelerating in conflict-affected territory with high population movement. This deserves more attention than it is currently receiving.

Outside the Frame

Bangladesh: 528 children dead from measles. The world has moved on before it arrived.

Since mid-March, Bangladesh has recorded over 60,000 suspected measles cases and 528 deaths — the vast majority children under five, with infants under nine months disproportionately represented. The case fatality rate is around 1%, compared to 0.1–0.3% in high-income countries. The Lancet published a piece this week calling it an "immunisation emergency" driven by pandemic-era vaccination collapse and severe malnutrition, which converts a vaccine-preventable infection into a mass-casualty event.

KPBS ran a piece titled "More than 500 children have died in an outbreak that the world is virtually ignoring." That framing is accurate. The story has received almost no mainstream coverage — partly because measles is not novel, partly because Ebola is consuming the disease narrative, and partly because Bangladesh doesn't command editorial attention the way DRC does. But the death toll already exceeds many Ebola outbreaks that generated sustained global concern. The mechanism making this unusual — malnutrition turning a preventable disease lethal — is the same mechanism that will recur in other countries in the coming years. Worth paying attention to now, before it becomes unavoidable.
