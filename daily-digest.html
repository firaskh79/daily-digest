Today's Signal

The Iran Deal Is Signed — But the Hard Part Is Still Ahead

JD Vance confirmed this morning that the US-Iran framework agreement was signed digitally on Sunday — Trump, Vance, and Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf all put their names to it. The Strait of Hormuz will reopen, the US naval blockade is lifting, and a formal signing ceremony in Geneva is set for Friday. Markets have moved accordingly: Brent crude fell 4.9% to $83, Nasdaq is up 3%, and the FTSE is rallying on energy import relief.

But the full text still hasn't been released. What's confirmed: a 60-day technical negotiation window to resolve Iran's uranium stockpile — which, per the Carnegie Endowment, currently sits at 60% enrichment, a short technical step from weapons-grade. The deal's central question — whether Iran actually gives up its path to a bomb — isn't answered, it's been kicked down the road. Vance's framing is deliberately conditional: "sanctions relief for Tehran would depend entirely on compliance." Friday's ceremony in Geneva will be historic theatre. The real deal comes in 60 days.

The most important thing to watch right now isn't Netanyahu — who has been conspicuously muted, claiming he "saved Israel from annihilation" rather than attacking the deal — it's the Iranian domestic picture. Hardliners in Tehran and Mashhad are already in the streets shouting "Death to the compromiser" and "Araghchi, have shame, leave the country alone." The new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei, who replaced his father after the February 28 strikes, has not publicly blessed the deal. The fragility is on the Iranian side, not the American one.

Watch: Full deal text release this week. Khamenei Jr.'s public posture. IAEA access conditions. Whether Iranian hardliner protests spread beyond Tehran and Mashhad. Oil market reaction once Hormuz actually reopens — not just announces.

Outside the Frame

The Assassination Ban Is Gone — and the Deal Makes It Harder to Say So

With the deal signed and the relief rally underway, a thread that deserves holding: Just Security's piece on Khamenei's killing and "the perilous death of the assassination ban" has received almost no mainstream attention. The US and Israel killed a sitting head of state on February 28. That's the first time a Western democratic power has done that in the modern era. The deal signing doesn't retroactively answer the question the killing raised — it actually makes the question harder to ask, because the narrative has moved to "it worked." The precedent is now set and apparently endorsed: killing a supreme leader can be part of a diplomatic sequence that ends with a deal. That logic will travel.

The Carnegie Endowment's May framing deserves reading alongside it: Iran may conclude that its Hormuz leverage is sufficient deterrence to quietly rebuild its nuclear capabilities without the active program Khamenei was pursuing. The new supreme leader faces different incentives. The 60-day uranium clock is negotiating time, not resolution — and the party most likely to exploit any ambiguity is Tehran's new leadership structure, not its hardliners.
