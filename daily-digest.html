48 Hours to Geneva

The Iran-US deal signs Thursday — but the text is still hidden and the two sides are describing different agreements

The formal MOU signing in Geneva is two days away, and the closer it gets the more visible the gaps. Iran says the deal requires Israeli forces to leave Lebanon. The US hasn't confirmed that. Neither government has released the actual text. On the nuclear side, both sides have agreed to a freeze-in-place during the 60-day negotiation window, but what "freeze" means for an already-advanced enrichment programme is precisely what those 60 days are supposed to settle — and that is the clause hardliners in Tehran are protesting against, chanting "death to the compromiser" in Mashhad.

The Lebanon angle is the live wire before Thursday. Hezbollah continues to reject Israeli "freedom of movement" provisions in Lebanese territory. Lebanon is reportedly inside the MOU, but the provisions haven't been confirmed in detail. President Pezeshkian has now credited Mojtaba Khamenei with directly shaping the deal — a significant legitimation signal — but that doesn't mean Hezbollah takes its cues from Tehran on this. The Carnegie framing from May holds: this defers the nuclear question, it doesn't answer it. The deal is real. What it means is still in negotiation.

Makerfield votes — and the margin is narrower than the seat's design intended

Burnham enters polling day with a five-point lead over Reform UK's Robert Kenyon (Labour 49%, Reform 44%, Opinium). The constituency was vacated specifically to give Burnham this path to Parliament and then to the Labour leadership. A five-point margin in a post-Belfast-riots context, with a nationally activated Reform campaign, is not the comfortable lead the circumstances were meant to produce. A Reform win would be the most destabilising UK political event in months. A narrow Burnham win still accelerates the challenge to Starmer: 95+ MPs are already on record calling for his departure.

Outside the Frame

South Africa · what the World Cup coverage is missing

More than 8,000 Malawian nationals — the majority women and children — are stranded at a transit site in Durban, waiting for buses to take them home. Since April, a South African movement called "March and March" has organised anti-immigrant demonstrations that turned violent, targeting nationals from Malawi, Zimbabwe, Mozambique, Nigeria, Somalia, and Ethiopia. A June 30 deadline — circulated by mob organisers — has created a fear dynamic: leave by then or face violence. South Africa's government is trying to source 1,000 buses. Malawi has declared the repatriation a national humanitarian emergency. The displacement has contracted regional cross-border trade by 22%.

The World Cup irony is direct: South Africa's 2010 tournament was marketed globally as Africa's moment, a continental coming-of-age. Now the continent's largest economy is expelling its neighbours in the weeks that FIFA anthems celebrate African solidarity. Modern Ghana's piece "The Love Is Gone" names this connection explicitly. Pan African Visions has the structural analysis. Al Jazeera's June 11 report has the human detail. This is being tracked as a new thread from today.
