Iran / Hormuz — Changed

Rome talks stall; Trump draws a line; Brent jumps $21 to $103

The fifth round of US–Iran negotiations in Rome ended Saturday without a breakthrough. Iran's chief negotiator, parliament speaker Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf, made clear that Tehran will not compromise on what it calls national rights, and the nuclear programme is explicitly off the table in any initial framework. Iran's stated priority is ending the war "on all fronts including Lebanon" before nuclear talks can begin — a sequencing demand the US has rejected.

Trump responded publicly with an ultimatum, telling reporters there is a "50/50" chance the two sides reach a deal — and that he will decide within roughly 24 hours if not, with the implication being a return to military action. Xi Jinping has reportedly offered to help broker access to the Strait of Hormuz as part of the US–China summit framework reached earlier this month, adding a new diplomatic variable that neither Washington nor Tehran has fully acknowledged.

Markets read the stalemate without hesitation. Brent crude moved from around $82 yesterday to $103–104 a barrel today, touching $106 intraday before easing on renewed diplomatic hope. A roughly 25% single-session move. BP fell 2.73% on the FTSE; US gas prices are at near-record highs ahead of Memorial Day. The oil shock is now feeding back into the UK cost-of-living picture.

Watch: Trump's decision window closes Sunday. Any naval incident in the Strait or an airstrike announcement would be an immediate market and diplomatic shock. A deal — even a partial framework — would likely bring Brent back below $90 quickly.

Israel / Lebanon — Changed

Strikes kill 10 today including four paramedics; evacuation orders for 10 villages

Israeli airstrikes intensified across southern Lebanon on Saturday. Raids on Deir Qanoun al-Nahr, Abbasiyeh and Hanawiya in the Tyre district killed at least 10 people, including four paramedics — the targeting of emergency responders drew immediate international criticism. Evacuation orders were issued for residents of 10 named villages in the south. A separate Israeli soldier was killed in northern Israel by a Hezbollah drone launched from south Lebanon.

Lebanon's army commander, General Heikel, issued an unusually public statement urging soldiers to "maintain morale and readiness" and describing the Lebanese Armed Forces as the "solid barrier against civil strife" — language that reads as a signal that the institution is under pressure to hold together. French envoy Jean-Yves Le Drian, referenced in today's Annahar, is reportedly in Beirut with intelligence suggesting the situation may be about to deteriorate further; the nature of that intelligence has not been publicly disclosed.

The Annahar is also tracking a parallel political file: fresh US Treasury sanctions on Hezbollah and Amal MPs, and a judicial complaint implicating Lebanon's General Security directorate in the issuance of forged passports allegedly linked to Hezbollah–IRGC networks. Lebanon's total death toll since March 2 has now passed 3,073.

Watch: Today's casualty figures may still be rising. The Le Drian intelligence question — and whether France escalates its diplomatic intervention — is worth following closely. US sanctions on Lebanese state institutions are a new pressure point that could destabilise the government.

Mearsheimer — Changed

"Eating Humble Pie": the case the Iran war is already lost

Mearsheimer's May 20 Substack post argues that the US–Israel military campaign against Iran has failed on its own terms. The February 28 strikes damaged but did not destroy Iran's nuclear capability. Iran has not capitulated. The attempt to use military force to resolve the nuclear question has instead produced a more hardened Iranian negotiating position — illustrated today in Rome. His earlier post "Following in LBJ's Footsteps?" (May 12) drew the analogy to Vietnam: a war that politically destroyed its architect because it could not be won but also could not be abandoned without humiliation.

Four rapid posts in eight days suggest he sees this as a turning-point moment. His core argument: US military overreach has produced exactly the regional instability and oil shock it claimed to be preventing, and the administration now faces a binary — an escalatory face-saving gesture (more strikes) or a negotiated settlement that gives Iran more than it would have gotten before February. He considers the latter the least bad option; he expects the administration to choose the former.

Watch: If Trump strikes Sunday, Mearsheimer's "lost war" framing becomes politically explosive. If a deal is struck, the question is whether it gives Iran what it wanted — which would validate his entire argument retroactively.
