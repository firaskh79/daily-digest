Outside the Frame

Madagascar's Gen Z rupture — a story nobody is watching. While Europe's attention has been on Belfast and London, Madagascar has been living through a different kind of political disruption. A social-media-led Gen Z movement toppled former president Andry Rajoelina's government, producing a military-appointed transitional prime minister who is now struggling to incorporate the decentralized, leaderless protest movement into any governance structure. It is a direct parallel to the 2025 Bangladeshi uprising pattern — and to Togo, where a June 6th Movement (M66) of artists and bloggers has been meeting arrests this week.

The structural observation worth naming: sub-Saharan and Indian Ocean Gen Z movements are following a common playbook — viral mobilization, no formal leadership, rapid toppling of incumbents, and then an immediate problem of converting disruption into governing power. None of these stories receive sustained Western media attention until they produce a coup or a famine. Madagascar is at the governance-vacuum stage now. The question of what comes after the rupture is the one that matters — and it's the question the West never bothers to ask until it's too late.
