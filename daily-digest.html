Outside the Frame

The Alliance of Sahel States — a new thing the West is not watching

Three military juntas — Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso — formally left ECOWAS in January 2025 and have now constituted the Alliance of Sahel States, with centralized frameworks for diplomacy, defence, and development. In May 2026 they conducted confirmed joint airstrikes as a confederation. In June, Burkina Faso arrested prominent religious leader Imam Mohamed Ishaq Kindo — a sign of how far internal control apparatus is reaching. The UN reports three million internally displaced persons across the three countries. Both JNIM (al-Qaeda-affiliated) and Islamic State Sahel Province are demonstrating what ACLED calls "unprecedented tactical sophistication." Western diplomatic engagement is near-total absence.

The frame through which this gets discussed in London — when it gets discussed at all — is "Sahel instability" as a migration driver. That frame misses what's actually happening: a three-state confederation explicitly aligned with Russia (Wagner successor forces remain deployed), systematically breaking from Western institutional frameworks, while the population it governs absorbs the consequences. The Stimson Center's June 2026 briefing and ACLED's regional tracker are the honest places to follow this. Neither is a household source, which is part of the problem.
