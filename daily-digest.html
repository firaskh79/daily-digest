The Ukraine peace deadline arrives — and nothing happens

Today is the day. Back in February, Trump announced the US had given Ukraine and Russia a "June deadline" to reach a peace agreement. June is here. There is no agreement, no credible framework, and no sign either side is close to one. Russian forces launched over 400 drones and approximately 40 missiles at Ukrainian targets overnight. Ukraine's counteroffensive has recovered around 400 km² of territory since February, but the front is essentially static. The interesting question now is what Trump does with a deadline he set publicly and missed publicly — escalate pressure on both parties, walk away from mediation, or quietly let the deadline dissolve. His next move on Ukraine will be more telling than anything he has said about it so far.

Colombia goes right — and it's more complicated than another Milei moment

In Sunday's first round of Colombia's presidential election, far-right outsider Abelardo de la Espriella took 43.7% of the vote, significantly outperforming polls that had favoured the left's Iván Cepeda. The runoff is June 21. De la Espriella is a criminal defense lawyer who has never held public office; he ran on crime, mega-prisons, ending peace talks with rebel groups, and shrinking the state. The reflex comparison to Milei is understandable but slightly off — Milei was an economist with a theory; de la Espriella is a courtroom operator whose core appeal is law-and-order rather than libertarian economics. What's not ambiguous is the result: four years of Petro's leftist experiment ends with Colombia on the verge of electing someone almost exactly opposite. The race three weeks from now will be a genuine test of whether the 2022 pink tide in Latin America has definitively reversed.

Sudan Famine

June 1 matters here specifically: today is the first day of Sudan's lean season (June–September), the annual period when food shortages consistently worsen. With 19.5 million people in acute food insecurity, famine confirmed in four Darfur areas, 825,000 children under five expected to suffer severe acute malnutrition this year, and only 20% of the $2.9 billion humanitarian response plan funded — the lean season beginning today is not background context. It is the accelerator. The trajectory over the next three months will determine whether the current crisis becomes something far harder to contain.
Watch: Whether the funding gap narrows as lean season conditions intensify coverage, or whether the structural invisibility in Western media holds through the summer.

Outside the Frame

Syria's first parliament since Assad — June 8

One week from today, Syria's new parliament convenes for the first time since the fall of Bashar al-Assad in December 2024. The 210-member People's Assembly has 140 elected seats and 70 appointed by President Ahmed al-Sharaa. The composition is already drawing criticism: roughly 5% women (down from the 6–13% range under Assad — which is remarkable when that is the comparison point), few religious minorities, and a legislature structurally weighted toward the executive. This is what the post-Assad transition looks like in practice: not a clean democratic break but a managed reconstruction under an Islamist-rooted leadership that emerged from the armed opposition. The parliament will likely propose renaming itself, shedding the Assad-era "People's Assembly" branding. What it does on minority rights and constitutional structure will be the more consequential story. Source: Asharq Al-Awsat, which had this ahead of the event.
