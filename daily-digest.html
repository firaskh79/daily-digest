Today's Signal

Iran · Gulf · Oil

The US military struck the M/T Settebello in the Gulf of Oman today — a precision hit on the engine room of an Italian-flagged tanker allegedly transporting Iranian oil. Three Indian crewmembers are missing; 21 were rescued. This is the eighth vessel disabled since the US began blockading Iranian ports in mid-April. Brent jumped 3.4% to $94.60.

The structural tension is now unmissable: the US is simultaneously running a naval blockade that kills crews and pursuing a nuclear deal with Iran, with the next round of talks on the calendar for the June 22 week. Every ship disabled is both coercion and deal-wrecker. The collateral cost today landed on India — whose crew are dead or missing — a country that has maintained careful neutrality and is the world's largest buyer of discounted Russian oil. If New Delhi responds with something more than a diplomatic protest, that neutrality may shift.

Taiwan · Strait · Quietly Significant

While every editorial eye was on the Gulf, Taiwan fired HIMARS rockets into the Taiwan Strait for the first time in history — directed toward China — in a live-fire drill in Taichung City today. The timing deserves attention: Trump paused the follow-on HIMARS arms deal with Taiwan after his Xi summit last month. Taiwan just demonstrated, in public, a capability whose future supply is in diplomatic limbo. Whether this was a signal to Washington or Beijing, it was a signal. It is getting almost no coverage in UK media. It should.

Ebola · DRC · Accelerating

The Ebola Bundibugyo case count in the DRC has risen to 598 confirmed with 115 deaths — up from 452 cases and 88 deaths just four days ago on June 6. That is roughly 37 new confirmed cases per day, and the rate has clearly accelerated. The US State Department announced an additional $20 million in funding today, bringing total US Ebola response to over $220 million, focused on preparedness in Burundi, Kenya, Rwanda, and South Sudan. Geographic spread beyond DRC and Uganda remains limited. The pace inside Ituri province is now the story to watch.

Outside the Frame

On the same day the US disabled a tanker in the Gulf of Oman, Taiwan fired HIMARS rockets into the Taiwan Strait for the first time — aimed toward China — during a live-fire drill in Taichung City. The drill was deliberate, public, and clearly signalling. The context: Trump paused Taiwan's follow-on HIMARS arms deal after his Beijing meeting with Xi last month. Taiwan just demonstrated, in front of cameras, a capability whose future supply Washington has put in diplomatic limbo.

With US bandwidth entirely consumed by the Iran conflict and the US stock market pricing in 4.2% inflation, the Taiwan signal received almost no airtime. That is precisely why it matters. The Iran war is not pausing China's calculus about Taiwan; if anything, US military overextension is a variable Beijing is actively calculating. The question of whether Taiwan can deter an attack does not pause because everyone is looking elsewhere.
