Makerfield: Britain is waiting

Polls closed at 10pm. Results expected from 3am, declared around 4:30. Burnham is ahead by 5 to 12 points depending on which pollster you trust — the range reflects how hard it is to model turnout in a by-election with this much national attention. But the more interesting data point surfaced this week: YouGov found that Burnham's national net favourability has fallen to −11 over the course of the campaign, even as he's been installed as Labour's presumptive saviour. Reform leads Labour 42–34 in general election intention nationally. Winning Makerfield clears Burnham for Westminster — it doesn't answer the harder question of whether he can actually beat Nigel Farage at a general election. Tonight is the beginning of a story, not its resolution.

Watch for: margin of victory over Reform UK's Robert Kenyon; whether Burnham files a formal leadership challenge within 48 hours; Starmer's response; whether Labour significantly underperforms the polls.

Geneva eve: an MOU is not a peace deal

The Iran-US memorandum of understanding gets signed tomorrow at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland. JD Vance is attending; Trump may join him. Brent has already priced in the signing — down from $83 to $79.49 this week, the largest single-week oil drop since the war began. But the Foundation for Defense of Democracies published a pointed counter-read yesterday under the precise headline "An MOU is not a peace deal." Their argument: Iran's foreign minister Araghchi has published his negotiating philosophy — that if a diplomatic outcome matters more to Washington than to Tehran, Tehran wins. Iran goes into tomorrow with no operating enrichment plants and 85% of its defence-industrial base destroyed. FDD reads that as leverage squandered, not leverage preserved. The 60-day nuclear talks that follow the signing are the real test.

The most consequential shift today is Hezbollah's. Yesterday they were explicitly rejecting deal provisions; today they're calling it a "major achievement" and, per Times of Israel sources, expecting a large cash injection from Iran once the MOU is sealed. This is the deal's internal logic made visible: Iran's partners get funded, and the question becomes whether that's a feature or a bug from Washington's perspective. Israel's defence minister is still publicly saying Israel will not withdraw from Lebanese territory. June 22 talks are on the calendar.

Watch for: whether any deal text goes public before the ceremony; Trump's presence or absence in Geneva; Hezbollah's formal statement post-signing; Israel's reaction to the public ceremony.

England 4–2 Croatia: it's on

Last night in Arlington, England opened the World Cup with their most convincing group-stage performance in years. Harry Kane scored twice in the first half, equalling the English record for World Cup goals. Jude Bellingham scored what is being reported as the fastest goal of any player in World Cup history, then added another seconds into the second half. Croatia levelled at 2–2 before the break, which reminded everyone why England games are always complicated, but Bellingham restored the lead immediately after half-time and Rashford sealed it. The cultural mood around the England squad is — cautiously, with all previous experience as warning — genuinely high. Next game is June 23 vs Panama.

Watch for: England vs Panama June 23; whether the chart registers this as a full cultural moment next week; Kane's injury status.

The Social / Cultural Layer

Two unrelated events landed in the same cultural window this week — England's World Cup opener and the Stranger Things series finale — and the chart is processing both simultaneously. The result is one of the more legible mood documents the chart has produced in months, and the divergence from what you'd expect is the signal.

England beat Croatia 4–2 last night in a match that produced genuinely euphoric moments. But the chart response to that isn't triumphant anthem energy. The biggest mover this week is Fleetwood Mac. "Everywhere" is up 26 positions. "Landslide" entered the UK chart for the first time in the 51 years since it was written — driven by its use in the Stranger Things finale. "Go Your Own Way," "Little Lies," and "The Chain" are all rising. The Screen Rant piece tracking this is calling it the "2026 Resurgence of Rumours," but the mechanism is the finale placement, not a general Fleetwood Mac revival: one deeply emotional TV moment triggered a wave.

Running alongside that: Kings of Leon "Sex on Fire" +12, The Killers "Mr. Brightside" +4 (the song England fans sing at tournaments), Justin Bieber "Sorry" +26, Ed Sheeran catalogue tracks climbing across the board, Elton John "Rocket Man" +17. This is a 2000s/2010s nostalgia wave — but it's not random. It tracks precisely with the emotional register of a generation watching both their football team do something unexpected and a defining cultural artefact of their adolescence end.

The divergence worth naming: Olivia Dean holds seven positions in the Apple Music UK top 120, including the number one spot where she's been frozen for weeks. The top five hasn't moved. The World Cup official song Shakira & Burna Boy's "Dai Dai" has over 1 billion views on TikTok and is generating 47,000 new videos a day globally. That tournament energy is real and enormous. But in the UK chart specifically, people are reaching for music that makes them feel something old and safe, not something new and celebratory. The feeling isn't "we've won" — it's "we've been waiting for something to feel good about for a long time, and now it's here, and it's complicated."

One more TikTok signal: "Fractures of Reality" by Aetherion Soundscapes — an ambient instrumental with no lyrics, no artist profile — is the fastest-growing audio this week at +43.5% UGC videos per day. 447,000 videos using a sound called "Fractures of Reality." That kind of ambient-sound explosion on TikTok typically tracks collective anxiety that doesn't yet have a language. The eve of the Geneva signing is plausible context.

Outside the Frame

A story not on the current map

While Geneva dominates Western attention, Russia signed a nuclear energy cooperation agreement with Laos on June 15 — Rosatom CEO Likhachev and the Laotian trade minister, in the presence of both prime ministers — and Laos simultaneously joined the OSJD rail corridor linking Southeast Asia to Europe via China, Central Asia, and Russia. Laos becomes a transit hub for a freight network that bypasses every Western-controlled trade route. This isn't an isolated move: Russia's Pivot to Asia blog tracks a systematic pattern of Rosatom building nuclear client states across the Global South while Western attention is directed at the Middle East. The Laos play is notable specifically because it combines energy dependence with infrastructure lock-in in one agreement. None of this reverses what happens in Geneva tomorrow. It's a parallel architecture being built. Worth following: The Mekong Memo (August Whitcraft's Substack, granular Laos/Mekong coverage) and Russia's Pivot to Asia (tracks the structural pattern across the region).
